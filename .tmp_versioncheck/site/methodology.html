Methodology

What the system predicts

For each ticker and horizon H, the model estimates: "What is the probability that |price return| >= threshold over the next H trading days?" This is a two-sided (direction-agnostic) large-move probability.

Event definition

An event occurs when |P(t+H)/P(t) - 1| >= threshold, where P(t) is the closing price on the forecast date and P(t+H) is the closing price exactly H trading days later. The threshold used for resolution is always the threshold stored at forecast time, not any later threshold.

Trading-day resolution

Resolution uses the actual trading calendar, not naive calendar-day arithmetic. H=5 means 5 trading days (one week), H=10 means 10 trading days (two weeks). Weekends and market holidays are skipped.

No-lookahead guarantee

The forecast ledger is append-only. Each forecast is written with a UTC timestamp before the resolution date. The resolution ledger is a separate file — forecast records are never modified after publication. Anyone can verify the temporal ordering by inspecting the raw JSONL files.

Model versioning

Each forecast records its model_version, git_commit, config_hash, and checkpoint_hash. The overview page groups metrics by model_version, showing the main model and baselines side by side. The site can also be built filtered to a single version with --version. Pooled metrics across all versions are shown at the top; per-version breakdowns below.

Calibration methodology

The underlying model uses GJR-GARCH volatility estimation, Monte Carlo simulation with Student-t fat tails and Merton jump-diffusion, followed by multi-feature online recalibration with histogram post-calibration. Full details are in the paper and METHODOLOGY.md.

Limitations

  • Small sample sizes: Live verification starts with zero resolved forecasts. Statistical conclusions require at minimum 50–100 resolved forecasts per group. Do not treat early metrics as definitive.
  • Overlapping predictions: Forecasts at different horizons from the same date share price information. Effective sample size (N_eff) is lower than raw count.
  • Simplified live engine: The live prediction engine is a research prototype. It does not include all features of the backtest (e.g., implied-vol blending, earnings proximity, regime routing).
  • No guarantee of stationarity: The model's backtest performance does not guarantee future performance. Market regimes change.
  • Data dependency: Forecasts depend on price data quality and availability. Data delays or errors affect both forecasts and resolutions.

What the live record can prove

  • Whether forecasts were published before outcomes were known (timestamp audit)
  • Whether predicted probabilities are calibrated over time (ECE, reliability curve)
  • Whether the model has skill vs. climatology (BSS)
  • Whether the model discriminates events from non-events (AUC)

What the live record cannot prove

  • That the model will continue to work in future market regimes
  • That the model is optimal or best-in-class
  • That the economic value of the forecasts exceeds trading costs
  • Superiority over baselines in real time (live baseline tracking is not yet implemented; baseline comparisons are available only in the backtest paper results)
